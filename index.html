The 10YHP: what this means for the NHS Workforce


In July the new 10 Year Health Plan was published, setting the direction to reform the NHS through 3 radical shifts:

  • Hospital to community

  • Analogue to digital

  • Sickness to prevention


The top 6 implications of the 10YHP for workforce:

  • Radically reduce dependency on IR: Ambition to lower international recruitment to less than 10% of new recruits by 2035.

  • Fewer but better-treated workforce: Fewer staff by 2035 than projected in the 2023 Long-Term Workforce Plan, but with the aim to give every staff member a personalised career development plan and use AI to reduce administrative burdens.

  • Empowering leaders and rewarding performance: Pay for senior managers to be linked to performance on care timeliness and financial management and rewards for high-performing staff will be available.

  • Future-proofing skills and training: Education and training to include comprehensive training in AI and digital tools. The plan will also create 1,000 new specialty training posts in areas of greatest need.

  • Modernising employment and contracts: To reform employment contracts in partnership with trade unions, to incentivise high-quality, productive care and to establish new minimum standards for modern employment by April 2026.

  • Changing roles to meet the community shift: Neighbourhood Health Services bringing care into the community requires new multidisciplinary teams, new integrated roles and a different skills mix.


From the think tanks

Think tanks react to the 10YWHP…

Think tanks support the vision of the 10 Year Health Plan but have serious doubts about whether its goals can be achieved. There is a shared concern that the plan is ambitious but lacks a realistic delivery strategy, especially given current financial pressures. The Nuffield Trust and the Health Foundation point out that while aims like shifting care into the community are correct, the plan does not have the significant funding that supported previous NHS reforms. The King’s Fund adds that the plan should have focused more on fixing basic problems. For example, new technology like AI scribes can only improve NHS productivity if staff do not have to spend 30 minutes every morning logging into multiple, outdated IT systems.

 

      Exploring the earnings of NHS doctors in England

The Nuffield Trust explored some key metrics related to resident doctors’ pay. They estimate that even after accounting for these latest uplifts, average real-terms earnings for 2025/26 still fall behind 2010/11 levels by between 4% and 10%. However, the position has significantly improved since 2023/23, despite high inflation and doctors sacrificing pay to go on strike. Looking back further, the following chart shows the trend in doctors’ pay since 1990. The chart indicates the pay settlements each year to explore whether pay has eroded over time. The trend reveals sustained periods of real-terms increases followed by similarly sustained periods of real-terms decreases in pay. This is  problematic because people are loss-adverse and feel the strain of real-terms cuts acutely, compared to the increases.

How much do pay increases cost?


A 1% uplift to doctors’ 2025/26 earnings is equivalent to a direct overall salary cost of around £160 million. This includes £93 million for a 1% uplift for consultants and £51 million for resident doctors. Therefore, in terms of direct salary costs, the announced 2025/26 uplift will add around £690 million in direct salary costs. Read more>>>

 

Potential government proposals to improve resident doctor retention: loans, pensions or pay?

Analysis by the Institute for Fiscal Studies (IFS) critically examined the potential government proposals to improve retention of resident doctors through student loan forgiveness and pension changes. The IFS argues that forgiving student loan debt is a poorly targeted policy that would not increase take-home pay for most doctors for many years, if at all, and would fail to benefit then early one-third of resident doctors who trained abroad. In contrast, the IFS emphasises the potential benefit of rebalancing remuneration away from generous but inflexible pensions and towards higher take-home pay. The article highlights that employees in the private sector receive a much lower share of compensation in the form of pension contributions – and therefore receive a higher share in the form of take-home pay. Interestingly, it is argued this could make the NHS pay package more attractive, especially to younger staff, at no long-run cost to the government, though it would increase spending in the short term. It could also reduce the number of resident doctors who choose to opt out of the NHS pension scheme as they cannot afford to make the employee pension contribution. Read on to find out more>>>

NIHR publications

Consequences, costs and cost-effectiveness of workforce configurations in English acute hospitals

A new study conducted by Griffiths et al. reveals that the mix of staff in English hospitals can be a matter of life or death for patients. Researchers found that having fewer doctors and allied health professionals was linked to an increased risk of death. In contrast, higher numbers of nurses on the wards led to better patient experiences and improved staff wellbeing.


The study took a closer look at four hospitals and found that on days with a shortage of registered nurses or nursing assistants, patients had a higher risk of dying and faced longer hospital stays. The researchers recommended that addressing registered nurse shortages is more crucial than fixing gaps in assistant staff. They also noted that while using temporary staff to fill these gaps is helpful, it is a more expensive and less effective solution than employing permanent staff. Read more>>>


Other UK publications

Understanding doctors’ decision making regarding migrating to the UK

The GMC commissioned research into the migration decisions of more than 3,000 overseas-qualified doctors who recently joined the UK medical register. Using segmentation analysis, the researchers identified 6 groups within this population, based on their attitudes and expectations when considering moving to the UK. These were:

  • Short-term hoppers: Highly mobile, likely to leave the UK within a year of moving here. Most important factor is speaking the local language.

  • Sceptical considerers: Low expectations of the UK but place importance on moving somewhere with family ties; work benefits and cost of living matter.

  • Family-focused professionals: Prioritises opportunities for family, salary and benefits less important.

  • Young pragmatists: Youngest segment, ease of moving and settling in key for this group, looking to have career progression opportunities.

  • Ambitious expecters: High expectations of the UK, ambitious and expect good remuneration and conditions in the UK.

  • Modern system seekers: Importance of working in advanced healthcare system, where their development and working environment will improve.

When considering where to migrate, the UK was perceived to be stronger than other countries on practical factors, such as immigration and registration processes and the ease of securing a job. The UK was also competitive when it came to learning and development, and opportunities for career progression. However, the UK was perceived to be weaker than other countries in relation to other factors, including salary levels, cost of living and quality of life. Around half (51%) of doctors who moved to the UK felt that their expectations had been met. For those that didn’t feel their expectations had been met, this was most commonly attributed to heavy workloads, poor work-life balance and stress. Read more>>>

 

Which healthcare students are the most empathetic?

Empathy plays a crucial role in healthcare, affecting both patient outcomes and staff wellbeing. Although not the focus of this study, it is discussed that empathy is an adaptive and teachable skill, and increasingly integrated into medical, nursing, and midwifery curricula. Bauer et al. researched the empathy levels of different student professions and found that empathy levels varied between disciplines, with BSc Midwifery students having the highest scores and BSc Nursing students the lowest. Analysis revealed that empathy was positively associated with: openness, agreeableness, and having children. Conversely, students with non-medical training prior to university and higher Social Dominance Orientation (the belief that some groups should dominate others) showed lower empathy scores.


Differences between nursing and midwifery students, despite similar program structures and professional values, further highlight the influence of structural and institutional factors. Midwifery students received stipends and more consistent mentorship, while nursing students reported limited financial support, recognition, and career prospects. These factors likely contributed to higher levels of stress, and, therefore, reduced empathy. Read more>>>

How effective are retention strategies for district nurses, and who are targeted?

Researchers aimed to investigate the experience, perceptions, and effectiveness of retention strategies of district nurses and nursing associates. Over 60% of respondents to a survey reported experiencing strategies related to a safe working environment (75%), flexible work schedules (65%), well-being (64%) and professional development opportunities (60%). Nurses on lower pay grades were less likely than higher pay bands to report experiencing professional growth opportunities and involvement in service decision-making. Nurses working in affluent areas were more likely to report experiencing more types of retention strategies than those working in socio-economically deprived areas. Participants' views on effective strategies were mixed but attention to financial aspects (particularly travel costs),manageable workloads, flexibility in work scheduling plus tailored induction/support for those new to district nursing were reported as the most effective. Global publications

How does the Australian healthcare labour market operate, and compare to England?

A recent report on the Australian healthcare labour market highlights several key trends that are highly relevant for the England’s NHS workforce:

  • High reliance on international staff – A third(32%) of its doctors and a fifth (18%) of its nurses are foreign trained, much higher than OECD countries on average. This mirrors England’s situation.

  • Challenges in long-term care – Australia reports one of the highest uses of part-time (92%) and fixed-term contracts for its care workers among OECD countries. This reliance on less stable employment models, combined with a heavy dependence on informal carers, points to sustainability issues as the population ages.

  • The ‘Anchor institution’ model – the report discusses the concept of large healthcare organisations acting as ‘anchor institutions’ to boost local economies. This involves hospitals and health services deliberately using their role as major employers to create stable jobs, widen participation for local residents, and build career paths, something the NHS in England is aiming to embed.

  • Focus on rural and remote workforce – Australia's vast geography creates major challenges in providing equitable healthcare to rural and remote areas. Key strategies identified include targeted student admissions from rural backgrounds, locating training facilities closer to these areas, and enhancing the scope of practice for rural health workers. Is it ethical to recruit internationally? An Australian context

Research found that recruiting nurses from overseas helps with staff shortages in Australia but can make healthcare shortages worse in the nurses' home countries. The research shows Australia has strong standards for assessing the skills of these nurses to ensure patient safety.


However, more is needed to support nurses when they arrive and to encourage them to stay in their jobs. Better support and retention are essential for a stable workforce and consistent, high-quality patient care. Read more>>>
